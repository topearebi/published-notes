The importance of documentation

The importance of documentation

Jun 12, 202519 min read

Core Takeaway Message: Personal process documentation is important, it is easy, and if we start now, the benefits will be immense. Target Audience: Members of the department of digital IT at the University of Salford. Approximate Duration: 30 minutes Tone: Engaging, storytelling-focused, practical, forward-looking.

  • Slide Title: Unlocking Our Collective Expertise: The Power of Personal Process Notes & AI-Driven Insights

    • [Date of Presentation]

    • “My name is [Your Name], and I’m excited to share some thoughts on a topic I believe holds significant potential for us all: ‘Unlocking Our Collective Expertise’.”

    • “We’ll be looking at the power of personal process notes and how, eventually, we could even leverage AI to gain further insights.”

    • A clean, professional background. Perhaps an abstract image representing interconnectedness, knowledge, or a subtle University of Salford branding element if appropriate. A stylised key unlocking a network or brain icon.

  • Slide Title: Why We’re Here & What We’ll Explore

    • A fresh look at “documentation.”

    • Your role in shaping this.

  • Detailed Speaker Notes:

    • (Engaging hook) “How many of us have ever thought, ‘If only I knew how they did that,’ or ‘I wish I’d written down how I solved that tricky issue last time’?”

    • “Today, I want us to take a fresh look at ‘documentation’ – not as a chore, but as a powerful tool. We’ll explore the often-untapped power of our individual, everyday process knowledge.”

    • “We’ll discuss the immediate, tangible benefits this can bring – for our own efficiency, for smoother handovers, for easier onboarding, and for preserving critical know-how within our department.”

    • “Then, we’ll look a little further ahead at an exciting vision: how we might one day use AI to amplify these benefits, helping us make smarter strategic decisions and further streamline how we work.”

    • “And most importantly, I want to discuss how each of you can play a part in this, starting with some very simple steps.”

  • Visual Suggestion:

    • A roadmap or journey graphic with icons representing the key agenda points. Or a lightbulb icon turning on, symbolising new perspectives.

    • “I’m [Your Name], and I’m relatively new to our fantastic Digital IT team here at Salford, but I’m incredibly keen to contribute and learn from you all.”

    • “More formally, my academic journey, which includes two Master’s degrees, has allowed me to delve deeply into how organisations learn, manage information, and innovate. This led to several research papers, including one specifically titled ‘AI-Driven Knowledge Management Systems’.”

Slide 4: The Knowledge Around Us: Explicit vs. Tacit

  • Slide Title: Understanding Our Knowledge Landscape

  • Key Content Points:

    • Explicit Knowledge: Codified, documented (manuals, official guides). The “Know-What.”

    • Tacit Knowledge: Experiential, intuitive, hard to articulate. The “Know-How.”

    • Our official notes are great for explicit knowledge.

    • But what about the “secret sauce” – our collective tacit wisdom?

  • Detailed Speaker Notes:

    • “To understand the opportunity before us, it’s helpful to distinguish between two fundamental types of knowledge within any organisation, including ours.”

    • “First, there’s Explicit Knowledge. This is the knowledge that’s easily articulated, written down, codified, and shared. Think of our official departmental procedures, our system manuals, our shared knowledge base articles. It’s the essential ‘know-what’.”

    • “Then, there’s Tacit Knowledge. This is far more personal, deeply rooted in individual experience, intuition, and context. It’s the practical skills, the ‘tricks of the trade’, the ‘gut feelings’ about how to solve a novel problem, the efficient shortcuts we discover. It’s the invaluable ‘know-how’.”

    • “Our current departmental notes do an excellent job capturing explicit knowledge, and that’s vital.”

    • “But my question today is: how effectively are we capturing and sharing that vast reservoir of tacit wisdom – the ‘secret sauce’ that each of you brings to your roles every day?”

  • Visual Suggestion:

    • A two-part diagram. One side an open book or document icon (Explicit), the other a silhouette of a head with gears or a lightbulb inside (Tacit). Arrows showing current systems capture explicit well, but tacit is often uncaptured.

Slide 5: The Reality of Uncaptured Know-How (Interactive)

  • Slide Title: Ever Felt This? The Power of “Unwritten Rules”

  • Key Content Points:

    • Quick show of hands:

      • Faced a tricky, “off-script” task recently?

      • Possess unique solutions/shortcuts not in official guides?

    • This gap is where opportunity lies.

  • Detailed Speaker Notes:

    • “Let’s make this concept of uncaptured tacit knowledge a bit more concrete with a quick interactive moment.”

    • “By a quick show of hands – and please be honest! – how many of us, perhaps in the last month or so, have found ourselves tackling a task that was a bit ‘off-script’ from the standard procedure, and it gave you a bit more trouble or took longer than expected simply because you didn’t have that one specific piece of insight or prior experience? (Pause, observe hands, acknowledge).”

    • “Okay, quite a few of us. Now, another quick show of hands: how many of us have developed our own specific, hard-earned experience, a useful ‘trick’, or a shortcut for some of our regular tasks that isn’t written down in any official guide, but you know it saves time or solves a common problem effectively? (Pause, observe hands, acknowledge).”

    • “Thank you. That gap – between the challenges many of us just acknowledged facing, and the undocumented solutions and efficiencies that others possess – is precisely where personal process documentation can make a real, immediate difference. It’s about connecting those dots.”

  • Visual Suggestion:

    • Two simple, large question mark icons or thought bubble icons. Or silhouettes of people with raised hands. Keep it clean and focused on the audience’s participation.

Slide 6: Introducing: Personal Process Documentation

  • Slide Title: Personal Process Notes: Your Know-How, Documented

  • Key Content Points:

    • Concise notes on how you execute specific tasks.

    • Not novels, but practical, actionable guides for you (and potentially colleagues).

    • Strategic Focus (Not “Document Everything!“):

      • Critical/High-Impact tasks (projects, goals).

      • Complex or Infrequently Performed procedures.

      • Tasks with “Gotchas” or common pitfalls.

      • Likely for handover/delegation.

  • Detailed Speaker Notes:

    • “So, what’s the first step in bridging this gap? I propose something straightforward: Personal Process Documentation.”

    • “This is simply about each of us taking concise, practical notes on how we execute our specific tasks and projects. Think step-by-step guides for recurring tasks unique to your workflow, checklists for complex procedures, notes on troubleshooting common issues you encounter, or key contacts for specific parts of a process.”

    • “Crucially, this isn’t about writing novels or creating burdensome new official documents. It’s about creating practical, actionable notes primarily for your own benefit, which can then easily benefit others.”

    • “And I want to be absolutely clear: this isn’t about trying to document every single micro-action we take. That would be overwhelming and counterproductive. The key is to be strategic and focus our efforts. We should prioritise documenting processes or tasks that are:”

    • “Firstly, Critical or High-Impact – those directly tied to key projects, our individual performance goals, or core departmental objectives.”

    • “Secondly, Complex or Infrequently Performed procedures – where details are easily forgotten between instances.”

    • “Thirdly, tasks known to have ‘Gotchas’ or common pitfalls not covered in official guides.”

    • “And finally, tasks you anticipate needing to pass on or explain to others in the future.”

    • “It’s about targeted documentation that delivers the most value for the effort involved.”

  • Visual Suggestion:

    • An icon of a notebook and pen, or a stylised checklist. Bullet points on the slide could have small, relevant icons (e.g., target for critical, brain for complex, warning sign for gotchas, people icon for handover).

Slide 7: The Immediate Wins: For You, The Individual

  • Slide Title: What’s In It For You? (Starting Today!)

  • Key Content Points:

    • Enhanced personal efficiency & clarity.

    • Reduced cognitive load (less to remember!).

    • Easier to pick up tasks after a break.

    • Consistency in your own work.

    • A foundation for self-reflection & improvement.

  • Detailed Speaker Notes:

    • “Let’s talk about the immediate, tangible benefits of this simple practice, starting with what’s in it for you as an individual.”

    • “Firstly, it brings enhanced personal efficiency and clarity. Documenting your process forces you to think it through, often revealing simpler or better ways to do things.”

    • “It reduces your cognitive load. You don’t have to keep every intricate step of every process in your head. Your notes become your external brain.”

    • “This makes it much easier to pick up tasks after a break, whether that’s a holiday or just switching between different projects. You can get back up to speed much faster.”

    • “It promotes consistency in your own work, ensuring you don’t miss crucial steps, especially for tasks you don’t do every day.”

    • “And finally, the very act of documenting can be a powerful tool for self-reflection and identifying areas for your own process improvement.”

  • Visual Suggestion:

    • An icon of a person with a lightbulb above their head, or a smoothly turning gear. Focus on individual empowerment.

Slide 8: The Immediate Wins: Smoother Handovers & Onboarding

  • Slide Title: Powering Up Our Team: Handovers & Onboarding

  • Key Content Points:

    • Dramatically smoother handovers (holidays, role changes).

    • Reduced burden on colleagues taking over.

    • Faster, more effective onboarding for new hires/internal moves.

    • Captures the “how-to” beyond official manuals.

  • Detailed Speaker Notes:

    • “The individual benefits are compelling, but the positive impact quickly extends to our colleagues and the wider team, especially when it comes to handovers and onboarding.”

    • “Imagine how much smoother handovers could be – whether someone is going on annual leave, moving to a new role within the department, or even leaving the university. Their personal process notes provide an invaluable head-start for the person stepping in.”

    • “This significantly reduces the burden and stress on colleagues taking over tasks, allowing them to become productive much faster.”

    • “For new hires, or for existing staff moving into new roles within Digital IT, these notes can make their onboarding experience dramatically faster and more effective. They get access not just to the official manuals, but to the practical, day-to-day ‘how-to’ from experienced team members.”

    • “This is about capturing that crucial operational wisdom that often lives only in people’s heads.”

  • Visual Suggestion:

    • Two figures shaking hands or passing a baton. Or a “welcome” sign with an arrow showing an upward trajectory (faster onboarding). Icons representing different roles in IT (BA, Dev, Service Desk) connecting.

Slide 9: The Immediate Wins: Safeguarding Our “Generational Knowledge”

  • Slide Title: Protecting Our “Secret Sauce”: The Knowledge Cliff

  • Key Content Points:

    • Many organisations have long-serving, deeply knowledgeable staff.

    • Risk: “Generational Knowledge Cliff” if many depart around the same time.

    • Losing invaluable, hyperspecific “secret sauce.”

    • Personal documentation now helps retain this vital experience.

  • Detailed Speaker Notes:

    • “There’s another critical, immediate benefit, especially relevant for established organisations like ours that are fortunate to have many dedicated, long-term staff.”

    • “Over many years, invaluable, often highly specific operational knowledge – what we might call the ‘secret sauce’ that keeps things running optimally – naturally becomes concentrated with our most experienced colleagues.”

    • “A common pattern we observe is that these groups of long-serving staff, having shared similar career journeys, may also approach significant milestones, like retirement, within a similar general timeframe. This isn’t a criticism; it’s a natural lifecycle.”

    • “The risk, however, is what we could term a ‘Generational Knowledge Cliff.’ If several key individuals with this deep, tacit knowledge depart concurrently, the department can face a sudden and substantial loss of critical operational wisdom, creating a knowledge vacuum that’s incredibly hard to fill quickly.”

    • “By actively promoting and creating personal process documentation now, we provide a vital mechanism to capture and retain this invaluable, often unwritten, experience before it walks out the door. This safeguards our operational continuity and our collective know-how for the future.”

  • Visual Suggestion:

    • An image of a vault or a treasure chest, symbolising valuable knowledge. Or a graph showing a “cliff edge” with an arrow showing documentation as a safety net.

      • Encourage personal process note-taking.

    • Longer-Term (The AI Vision):

      • This is a conversation starter.

      • No immediate implementation plan for AI.

      • Interested in exploring this further? Let’s talk! (Form a working group?)

  • Detailed Speaker Notes:

    • “So, how do we move forward from here?”

    • “My short-term proposal is simple and actionable: let’s start now by simply encouraging and embracing the habit of creating these personal process notes. We could share a few simple templates or suggest preferred tools that many of us already use, like OneNote, or perhaps a dedicated space within our existing knowledge base for these personal drafts.”

    • “Perhaps we could even form a small, voluntary pilot group to actively try this for a month and then share their experiences and any useful templates they develop.”
